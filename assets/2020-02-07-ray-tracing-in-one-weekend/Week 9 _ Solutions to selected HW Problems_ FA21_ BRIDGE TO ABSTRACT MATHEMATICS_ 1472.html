    Section 3.2

    1. Determine which of the following relations are reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.

    g) ''divides'' on NN. 

    Solution. The relation 'divides' on NN is can be expressed as a subset R={(x,y)∈N×N:x divides y}R={(x,y)∈N×N:x divides y} of N×NN×N.

    Since for each x∈Nx∈N, we have x=1.xx=1.x, so xx divides xx. Therefore, the relation 'divides' (or R)R) is reflexive on NN.

    Let x,y∈Nx,y∈N such that xx divides yy. Then y=kxy=kx for some integer kk, so x=(1/k)yx=(1/k)y, where 1/k1/k is not necessarily an integer (unless k=1k=1). Thus it is not necessarily true that yy divides xx whenever xx divides yy. Or we may just give a counterexample, if x=1x=1 and y=2y=2, then xx divides yy, but yy does not divide xx. Hence RR is not symmetric on NN. 

    Finally, let x,y,z∈Nx,y,z∈N such that xx divides yy and yy divides zz. Then there exists integers mm and nn such that y=mxy=mx and z=nyz=ny, so z=n(mx)=mnxz=n(mx)=mnx, where mnmn is again an integer, so xx divides zz. Hence RR is transitive on NN. □◻

    h) {(x,y)∈Z×Z:x+y=10}{(x,y)∈Z×Z:x+y=10}.

    Solution. Let R={(x,y)∈Z×Z:x+y=10}R={(x,y)∈Z×Z:x+y=10}. Then R is a relation on ZZ because RR is a subset of Z×ZZ×Z.

    For every x∈Zx∈Z, it is not always true that x+x=10x+x=10 so that it is not always true that (x,x)∈R(x,x)∈R. For instance, if x=1x=1, then x+x=2≠10x+x=2≠10, so (x,x)∉R(x,x)∉R. Thus R is not reflexive on ZZ. 

    Let x,y∈Zx,y∈Z such that (x,y)∈R(x,y)∈R. Then x+y=10x+y=10, which can be expressed as y+x=10y+x=10, so (y,x)∈R(y,x)∈R. Hence RR is symmetric on ZZ.

    Let x,y,z∈Zx,y,z∈Z such that (x,y)∈R(x,y)∈R and (y,z)∈R(y,z)∈R. Then x+y=10x+y=10 and y+z=10y+z=10, but this does not necessarily imply that x+z=10x+z=10 so that (x,z)(x,z) is in RR. For example (counterexample), if x=2x=2, y=8y=8 and z=2z=2, then x+y=10=y+zx+y=10=y+z, so (x,y),(y,z)∈R(x,y),(y,z)∈R but (x,z)∉R(x,z)∉R since x+z=4≠10x+z=4≠10. Therefore, RR is not transitive on ZZ. □◻

    Note: Keep in mind that a counterexample is always enough to show that a result is not necessarily true.

    15 a). Suppose that RR and SS are equivalence relations on a set AA. Prove that R∩SR∩S is an equivalence relation on AA. 

    Proof. Note that given equivalence relations RR and SS are reflexive, symmetric and transitive on AA.

    Let x∈Ax∈A. Since RR and SS on AA are reflexive on AA, (x,x)∈R(x,x)∈R and (x,x)∈S(x,x)∈S. Thus (x,x)∈R∩S(x,x)∈R∩S. Therefore, R∩SR∩S is reflexive on AA.

    Let x,y∈Ax,y∈A such that (x,y)∈R∩S(x,y)∈R∩S. Then (x,y)∈R(x,y)∈R and (x,y)∈S(x,y)∈S. Since RR and SS are both symmetric, (y,x)∈R(y,x)∈R and (y,x)∈S(y,x)∈S. Thus (y,x)∈R∩S(y,x)∈R∩S. Therefore, R∩SR∩S is symmetric on AA. 

    Let x,y,z∈Ax,y,z∈A such that (x,y),(y,z)∈R∩S(x,y),(y,z)∈R∩S. Then (x,y),(y,z)∈R(x,y),(y,z)∈R and (x,y),(y,z)∈S(x,y),(y,z)∈S. Since RR and SS are both transitive, (x,z)∈R(x,z)∈R and (x,z)∈S(x,z)∈S. Thus (x,z)∈R∩S(x,z)∈R∩S. Therefore, R∩SR∩S is transitive on AA. 

    Hence R∩SR∩S is an equivalence relation on AA. □◻
